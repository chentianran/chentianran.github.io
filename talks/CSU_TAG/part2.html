Adjacency polytopes

Given a connected graph $G$, represented by its adjacency/edge list $A$ (list of edges and loops), we consider a function \[ f : A \to \mathcal{P}^n \] where $\mathcal{P}^n$ is the set of convex (lattice) polytopes in $\mathbb{R}^n$.

The adjacency polytope of $G$, induced by $f$, is the (lattice) polytope \[ P = \operatorname{conv} \left( \{ \mathbf{0} \} \cup \bigcup_{ \{i,j\} \in A } f(\{i,j\}) \right) \; \subset \; \mathbb{R}^n \]

(It is defined only up to a unimodular transformations.)

Example.

\begin{align*} A &= \{\; \{0,1\}, \{1,2\}, \{2,0\} \;\} \\ f(\{i,j\}) &= \operatorname{conv} \{ \mathbf{e}_i - \mathbf{e}_j, \mathbf{e}_j - \mathbf{e}_i \} \end{align*}

\[ P = \operatorname{conv} \{ \pm (\mathbf{e}_0 - \mathbf{e}_1), \pm (\mathbf{e}_1 - \mathbf{e}_2), \pm (\mathbf{e}_2 - \mathbf{e}_0) \} \]

There are some interesting connections between the $G$ and $P$.

Circuit facets $\to$ Spanning chordless cycles
Facets $\leftrightarrow$ Max. bipartite subgraphs

Kuramoto model

Standard model for studying spontaneous synchronizations in networks of weakly coupled oscillators (Yoshiki Kuramoto 1975).

Mechanical model: Massless particles rotating on the unit circle coupled together by springs.

Each particle has its own natural frequency. The strength of influence of one particle on another is proportional to the sine of the phase angle difference.

\[ \dot{\theta}_i = \omega_i - \sum_{j=0}^n k_{ij} \sin(\theta_i - \theta_j) \quad\text{for } i=0,\dots,n \] Kuramoto Y. (1975) Self-entrainment of a population of coupled non-linear oscillators. In: Araki H. (eds) International Symposium on Mathematical Problems in Theoretical Physics. Lecture Notes in Physics, vol 39. Springer, Berlin, Heidelberg.

Frequency synchronization

Configurations of $(\theta_0,\dots,\theta_n)$ for which $\dot{\theta}_i$ are all the same

\[ c = \omega_i - \sum_{j=0}^n k_{ij} \sin(\theta_i - \theta_j) \quad\text{for } i=0,\dots,n \]

We can reduce this into a square system of $n$ equations \[ 0 = \bar{\omega}_i - \sum_{ j \in \mathcal{N}_G(i) } k_{ij} \sin(\theta_i - \theta_j) \quad\text{for } i=1,\dots,n \] in $n$ real variables $\theta_1,\ldots,\theta_n$.

Through the change of variables and the change of variables $ x_i = e^{r_i + \mathbf{i}\,\theta_i} $ we get system of $n$ Laurent polynomials \[ \bar{\omega}_i - \sum_{ j \in \mathcal{N}_G(i) } a_{ij}' \left( \frac{x_i}{x_j} - \frac{x_j}{x_i} \right) \] in the $n$ complex variables $x_1,\ldots,x_n$.

Randomization and perturbation

For numerical stability considerations, we usually do not directly solve \[ p_i (\mathbf{x}) = \bar{\omega}_i - \sum_{ j \in \mathcal{N}_G(i) } a_{ij}' \left( \frac{x_i}{x_j} - \frac{x_j}{x_i} \right) =0, \quad i = 1,\ldots,n. \]

Instead, we solve the "randomized" and perturbed version \[ \begin{bmatrix} q_1 (\mathbf{x}) \\ \vdots \\ q_n (\mathbf{x}) \end{bmatrix} = M \cdot \begin{bmatrix} p_1 (\mathbf{x}) + \epsilon_1 \\ \vdots \\ p_n (\mathbf{x}) + \epsilon_n \end{bmatrix} = \mathbf{0} \] where the nonsingular matrix $M$ is chosen carefully with some randomness.

\[ q_k(\mathbf{x}) = c_{k,0,0} - \sum_{ \{i,j\} \in E } \left( c_{k,i,j} \frac{x_i}{x_j} - c_{k,j,i} \frac{x_j}{x_i} \right), \quad k=1,\ldots,n. \]

Three flavors

  • Original real system $P(\boldsymbol{\theta})$ \[ \bar{\omega}_i - \sum_{ j \in \mathcal{N}_G(i) } k_{ij} \sin(\theta_i - \theta_j) \]
  • Complex Laurent system $P(\mathbf{x})$ \[ \bar{\omega}_i - \sum_{ j \in \mathcal{N}_G(i) } a_{ij}' \left( \frac{x_i}{x_j} - \frac{x_j}{x_i} \right) \]
  • Randomized and perturbed Laurent system $Q(\mathbf{x})$ \[ c_{k,0,0} - \sum_{ \{i,j\} \in E } \left( c_{k,i,j} \frac{x_i}{x_j} - c_{k,j,i} \frac{x_j}{x_i} \right). \]

Root counting

  • Original real system $P(\boldsymbol{\theta})$
  • Complex Laurent system $P(\mathbf{x})$
  • Randomized Laurent system $Q(\mathbf{x})$

Root counting problem: How many isolated roots can there be?

  • Real roots of $P(\boldsymbol{\theta})$ corresponds to synchronization configurations.
  • Real roots of $P(\boldsymbol{\theta})$ corresponds to complex roots of $P(\mathbf{x})$.
  • Complex roots of $P(\mathbf{x})$ may not correspond to real roots of $P(\boldsymbol{\theta})$.
  • Complex roots of $P(\mathbf{x})$ are close to complex roots of $Q(\mathbf{x})$.

Generic root count of $Q(\mathbf{x})$

For generic choices of coefficients, what is complex root count of... \[ q_k(\mathbf{x}) = c_{k,0,0} - \sum_{ \{i,j\} \in E } \left( c_{k,i,j} \frac{x_i}{x_j} - c_{k,j,i} \frac{x_j}{x_i} \right) \quad k = 1,\ldots,n ? \]

Kushnirenko's Theorem. The number of isolated $\mathbb{C}^*$-roots $(q_1,\ldots,q_n)$ has is less than or equal to the normalized volume of the common Newton polytope of $q_1,\ldots,q_n$.

For generic choices of the coefficients, this root count is exact.

The generic root count of $Q(\mathbf{x})$ (the randomized and perturbed system) is the normalized volume of the adjacency polytope \[ \operatorname{conv} \left( \bigcup_{ \{i,j\} \in E } \{ \mathbf{e}_i - \mathbf{e}_j \;,\; \mathbf{e}_j - \mathbf{e}_i \} \cup \{ \mathbf{0} \} \right) \]

Bounding the root count of $P(\mathbf{x})$

Can we bound the root count of the system \[ p_i(\mathbf{x}) = \bar{\omega}_i - \sum_{ j \in \mathcal{N}_G(i) } a_{ij}' \left( \frac{x_i}{x_j} - \frac{x_j}{x_i} \right), \quad i = 1 \ldots,n \, ? \]

Bernshtein's First Theorem. The number of isolated $\mathbb{C}^*$-roots the system $P = (p_1,\ldots,p_n)$ has is bounded by the mixed volume of the Newton polytopes of $P$ (BKK bound).

\[ \text{BKK bound} = \operatorname{MV}(NP_1,\ldots,NP_n) \] where \[ NP_i = \operatorname{conv} \left( \{ \mathbf{0} \} \cup \{ \pm(\mathbf{e}_i - \mathbf{e}_j) \mid j \in \mathcal{N}_G(i) \} \right) \]

There appear to be no published results on direct computations of this bound for the Kuramoto model.

Is there a gap between this two bounds?


For many graphs, No!

Proposition. If $G$ is a tree, cycle, or $G$ can be decomposed into trees and cycles at cut vertices, then \[ \operatorname{MV}(NP_1,\ldots,NP_n) = \operatorname{Vol}_n(AP) \]

Conjecture. This is true for all graphs. (Appears to be proved)

Monotonicity conditions of mixed volume.

Practical implications: The AP bound $\operatorname{Vol}_n(AP)$ appears to be much easier to compute. And it leads to a particularly efficient algorithm for compute finding all complex roots.

BKK bound revisited

The BKK bound for the system \[ p_i(\mathbf{x}) = \bar{\omega}_i - \sum_{ j \in \mathcal{N}_G(i) } a_{ij}' \left( \frac{x_i}{x_j} - \frac{x_j}{x_i} \right), \quad i = 1 \ldots,n, \] is the mixed volume \[ \operatorname{MV}(NP_1,\ldots,NP_n). \]

This bound is exact if coefficients are generic.

However, there are built-in relations among the coefficients: \begin{align*} &a'_{ij} \frac{x_i}{x_j} &&\text{and} & &a'_{ij} \frac{x_j}{x_i} \end{align*} must have the same coefficients. I.e., non-generic coefficients.

More precise generic root count

For generic choices of the coefficients $\overline{\omega}_i, a'_{ij}$, what is the root count for \[ p_i(\mathbf{x}) = \bar{\omega}_i - \sum_{ j \in \mathcal{N}_G(i) } a_{ij}' \left( \frac{x_i}{x_j} - \frac{x_j}{x_i} \right), \quad i = 1 \ldots,n \,? \]

A better question: Define spaces of rational functions \[ L_i = \operatorname{Span} \left\{ \frac{x_i}{x_j} - \frac{x_j}{x_i} \;:\; j \in \mathcal{N}_G(i) \right\} \cup \{ 1 \} \quad i = 1,\ldots,n. \]

For generic choices $\ell_i \in L_i$ for $i = 1,\ldots,n$, what is the number of intersections \[ \mathcal{V}(\ell_1) \,\cap\, \cdots \, \cap \, \mathcal{V}(\ell_n) \] in the algebraic torus $(\mathbb{C}^*)^n$ ? (Away from the base locus)

Birationally invariant intersection index

In this setup, we start with spaces of rational functions \[ L_i = \operatorname{Span} \left\{ \frac{x_i}{x_j} - \frac{x_j}{x_i} \;:\; j \in \mathcal{N}_G(i) \right\} \cup \{ 1 \} \quad i = 1,\ldots,n. \] Kaveh and Khovanskii developed the notation of birationally invariant intersection index \[ [L_1,\ldots,L_n] := | \mathcal{V}(\ell_1) \,\cap\, \cdots \, \cap \, \mathcal{V}(\ell_n) | \] which generalizes the BKK bound.

  • Kaveh, K., & Khovanskii, A. G. (2008). Mixed volume and an extension of intersection theory of divisors. arXiv:0812.0433
  • Kaveh, K., & Khovanskii, A. G. (2012). Newton-Okounkov bodies, semigroups of integral points, graded algebras and intersection theory. Annals of Mathematics, 176(2), 925–978.

This is a refinement of the BKK bound in the sense that \[ [L_1,\ldots,L_n] \le \operatorname{MV}(NP_1,\ldots,NP_n) \quad \text{(BKK bound)} \]

Is there a gap?

B.I.I.I vs BKK bound vs AP bound

Proposition. If $G$ is a complete graph, a tree, a cycle, or a graph that can be decomposed into trees and cycles at cut vertices, then \[ [ L_1, \ldots, L_n ] = \operatorname{MV}(NP_1,\ldots,NP_n) = \operatorname{Vol}_n(AP). \]

I.e., in these cases, \[ \text{B.I.I.I.} = \text{BKK bound} = \text{AP bound} \]


  • C. On the equality of BKK bound and birationally invariant intersection index. arXiv:1812.05408
  • C., R. Davis, D. Mehta, Counting equilibria of the Kuramoto model using birationally invariant intersection index. SIAM Journal on Applied Algebra and Geometry, 2018, Vol. 2, No. 4 pp. 489-507

Conjecture. This is true for all graphs. (Several cases appear to be proved)

What about real root count?

"Complex" synchronization configurations $(x_1,\ldots,x_n)$ corresponds to "real" configurations $(\theta_1,\ldots,\theta_n)$ via \[ x_i = e^{r_i + \mathbf{i} \theta_i}. \]

Only "complex" synchronization configurations with $|x_i| = 1$ are "real".

Here "real" means $x_i$'s are on the unit circle.

Constructive real root count results

Theorem. (J. Lindberg, A. Zachariah, N. Boston, B. Lesieutre, 2020) If $G$ is a cycle, then the number real synchronization configurations is \[ | \mathcal{V}_{\mathbb{R}} (P(\boldsymbol{\theta})) | = (n+1) \binom{n}{\lfloor n/2 \rfloor} = \operatorname{Vol}_n(AP). \]

J. Lindberg, A. Zachariah, N. Boston, B. Lesieutre, The Distribution of the Number of Real Solutions to the Power Flow Equations arXiv:2010.03069


Corollary. If $G$ is a tree, a cycle, or a graph that can be decomposed into trees and cycles at cut verticies, then \[ | \mathcal{V}_{\mathbb{R}} (P) | = [ L_1,\ldots,L_n ] = \operatorname{MV}(NP_1,\ldots,NP_n) = \operatorname{Vol}_n(AP). \]

Power-flow equations (Lossless PQ-formulation)

The power-flow (load-flow equations) equations describe the synchronization/balancing conditions of a power network consisting of $n+1$ buses (buses $0,1,\ldots,n$): \[ S_i = \sum_{j \in N_G(i)} \overline{Y}_{ij} v_i \overline{v}_j \quad\text{for } i = 1,\dots,n \]

It can be turned into a square polynomial system \[ \left\{ \begin{aligned} S_i &- \sum_{j \in N_G(i)} \overline{Y}_{ij} v_i u_j \quad\text{for } i = 1,\dots,n \\ \overline{S}_i &- \sum_{j \in N_G(i)} Y_{ij} u_i v_j \quad\text{for } i = 1,\dots,n \end{aligned} \right. \] T.-Y. Li, T. Sauer, and J. Yorke, Numerical Solution of a Class of Deficient Polynomial Systems, SIAM Journal of Numerical Analysis (1987)

Adjacency polytope of PQ-type power-flow equations

After a similar randomization process, the common Newton polytope is \[ AP = \operatorname{conv} \left( \left\{ \begin{bmatrix} \mathbf{e}_i \\ \mathbf{e}_j \end{bmatrix} , \begin{bmatrix} \mathbf{e}_j \\ \mathbf{e}_i \end{bmatrix} \,:\, \{i,j\} \in A \right\} \cup \{ \mathbf{0} \} \right) \subset \mathbb{R}^{2n}, \] where $A$ is the list of edges and loops (adjacency list).

Proposition. The adjacency polytope bound $\operatorname{Vol}_n(AP)$ can be computed by counting draconian sequences \[ a_{i_1} + \cdots + a_{i_k} < \left|\bigcup_{j=1}^k \mathcal{N}_G(i_j)\right|. \]
