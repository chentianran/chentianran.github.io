Power series

So far we studied series of numbers. The question we will be answering today is: Can we get some variables involved and use series to represent functions?

Tianran Chen

Department of Mathematics
Auburn University at Montgomery

Motivations

We studied geometric series of the form \[ \sum_{k=0}^\infty r^k = 1 + r + r^2 + r^3 + r^4 + \cdots \] and we now know exactly for which $r$ this series will converge.

A natural question to ask is then: Can we treat $r$ as a variable? And will that produce a function? This is what power series is all about.

Power series

They are expressions that look like polynomials with infinite number of terms.

A power series centered at $x=0$ (or about $x=0$) is an expression of the form \[ \sum_{k=0}^\infty c_k x^k = c_0 + c_1 x + c_2 x^2 + c_3 x^3 + \cdots \] where $c_0,c_1,c_2,\ldots$ are real constants known as coefficients.

In general, a power series centered at $x = a$ is an expression \[ \sum_{k=0}^\infty c_k (x-a)^k = c_0 + c_1 (x-a) + c_2 (x-a)^2 + c_3 (x-a)^3 + \cdots \] for some real constants (the coefficients) $c_0,c_1,c_2,\ldots$.

Simply put, a power series is an infinite series in which each term has a nonnegative power of an variable.

Does a power series define a function?

Keep in mind that we may not be able to assign meaningful value to it once we ``plug in'' a value for $x$ --- as we know by now, not all series have meaningful values. This is the question of convergence.

It is clear that a power series $\sum_{k=1}^\infty c_k(x-a)^k$ always converges at its center $x=a$: If we "plug in" $x=a$, we get \[ c_0 + c_1 (a-a) + c_2 (a-a)^2 + \cdots = c_0 + 0 + 0 + \cdots = c_0. \]

An important question we need to study is that given a power series centered at $x=a$, are there any other value we can plug in for $x$ and get a convergent series (and hence a meaningful value)?

Radius of convergence

Each power series centered at $x=a$ has a radius of convergence such that for any $x$ within this radius around its center, the series converges.

A power series $\sum_{k=0}^\infty c_k (x-a)^k$ has three possibilities:

  1. This series converges only at $x=a$ and diverges for all other $x$ values. In this case, we say this series has a radius of converges of 0.
  2. This series converges for all $x$ values. In this case, we say this series has a radius of converges of $\infty$.
  3. There exists a real $R > 0$ such that this series\ldots
    • converges for all $x$ such that $|x - a| < R$, and
    • diverges for all $x$ such that $|x - a| > R$.
    We call $R$ the radius of converges of this series.

In these cases, the set of real numbers for which the series converge form a interval, and it is called the interval of convergence of the power series.

Within this interval of convergence, power series having the same center can be manipulated algebraically, e.g., the can be added, and multiplied.

For example, \begin{align*} \sum_{k=0} b_k (x-a)^k + \sum_{k=0} c_k (x-a)^k &= \sum_{k=0} (b_k + c_k) (x-a)^k \\ b \cdot \sum_{k=0} c_k (x-a)^k &= \sum_{k=0} b c_k (x-a)^k \end{align*}

Power series as functions

When the interval of convergence consists of more than one number, within this interval, the power series defines a function that is continuous and differentiable. Indeed, we can show that all derivatives of this function exists and are continuous in that interval.

This also means, within their interval of convergence, power series can be differentiated and integrated term by term. \[ \frac{d}{dx} \sum_{k=0}^\infty c_k (x-a)^k = \sum_{k=0}^\infty \frac{d}{dx} \left[ c_k (x-a)^k \right] = \sum_{k=1}^\infty c_k k (x-a)^{k-1} \]

Analytic functions

Functions given by power series are called are called analytic functions.

Many familiar functions in calculus are analytic. For Example, \begin{align*} e^t &= \sum_{k=0}^\infty \frac{t^k}{k!} & \ln(1+t) &= \sum_{k=1}^\infty \frac{ (-1)^{k-1} }{k} t^k & \\ \cos(t) &= \sum_{k=0}^\infty \frac{(-1)^k t^{2k}}{(2k)!} & \sin(t) &= \sum_{k=0}^\infty \frac{(-1)^k t^{2k+1}}{(2k+1)!} \end{align*}

Historically, power series are the main weapons for mathematicians for a long time. It is likely that mathematicians thought that all "reasonable" functions are analytic. This assumption turn out to be quite wrong. Nonetheless, power series remain extremely useful in applied mathematics.
