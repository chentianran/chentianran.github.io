Gradients and differentials

The gradient vectors and differentials of functions in several variables

The gradient vector and differential of a real-valued function in several variables give us more insight into how such functions change in a small neighborhood.

Review: differentiability

Recall that partial and directional derivatives are rather "weak" concepts for modeling the local changes of a function in several variables. We mentioned a stronger condition called "differentiability".

A function $f$, in the $n$ variables $\mathbf{x} = (x_1,\dots,x_n)$, is said to be differentiable at a point $\mathbf{x}_0$ (in the interior of its domain) if there exists a linear map $J: \mathbf{R}^n \to \mathbf{R}$ such that \[ \lim_{\mathbf{h} \to \mathbf{0}} \frac{ |\, f( \mathbf{x}_0 + \mathbf{h} ) - f( \mathbf{x}_0 ) - J(\mathbf{h}) \,| }{ \| \mathbf{h} \| } = 0. \]

This is a very important concept. It is a direct generalization of the idea of differentiability for functions in one variables.

This condition ensures the function can be approximated, to arbitrary precisions, by a linear function near a point.

Today, we aim to gain a deeper understanding of the linear map $J$.

The case of two variables

In case where $f$ is a function in the two variables $x$ and $y$, the differentiability condition at $(x,y)$ is given by \[ \lim_{ (\Delta x,\Delta y) \to (0,0) } \frac{ | f( x + \Delta x, y + \Delta y ) - f( x, y ) - J(\Delta x,\Delta y) | }{ \sqrt{\Delta x^2 + \Delta y^2 }} = 0. \] where $J: \mathbf{R}^2 \to \mathbf{R}$ is a linear map.

In taking the limit as $(\Delta x,\Delta y) \to (0,0)$ we allow $(\Delta x,\Delta y)$ to approach $(0,0)$ from any direction, and the limit must be independent from choices of directions.

The term linear map has very specific meanings. Indeed the linear map $J: \mathbf{R}^2 \to \mathbf{R}$ must be of the form \[ J(\Delta x,\Delta y) = a \Delta x + b \Delta y \] for some real numbers $a$ and $b$. This should remind you of a dot product.

The linear approximation

From the view point of approximation, the condition of differentiability can also be understood as the condition that \[ f( x + \Delta x, y + \Delta y ) \approx f( x, y ) + J(\Delta x,\Delta y) \] as $\Delta x$ and $\Delta y$ become sufficiently small.

This approximation should remind you of the "linear approximation" formula for function in one variable \[ f(x + \Delta x) \approx f(x) + f'(x) \Delta x. \]

So, here, the linear map $J$ plays the role of the multiplication by the derivative.

Can visualize that, if we fix $x$ and $y$ and allow $\Delta x$ and $\Delta y$ to vary, the function \[ f( x, y ) + J(\Delta x,\Delta y) \] produce a plane in space?

Indeed, this plane is the tangent plane of the graph of $f$ at $(x,y)$.

The tangent plane

Indeed, the tangent plane at a point is exactly the plane that contains all the tangent lines at that point.

That is, in any cross section through that point and parallel to the $z$-axis (a vertical cross section), you will see a tangent line.

Differentiability is a stronger condition

The concept of differentiability we just defined is a strictly "stronger" condition than the existence of partial derivatives in the sense that being differentiable implies the existence of partial derivatives.

Let $f$ be a function in the $n$ variables. If $f$ is differentiable, then all its partial derivatives exist.

The converse is not true. We have seen non-differentiable functions that have all partial derivatives. But with the additional condition of continuity of partial derivatives, differentiability can be established.

Suppose $f$ be a function in $n$ variables $x_1,\dots,x_n$, and $\mathbf{p}_0$ is a point in the interior of its domain. If the partial derivative $\frac{\partial f}{\partial x_i}$ exists and is continuous at $\mathbf{p}_0$ for each $i$, then $f$ is differentiable at $\mathbf{p}_0$
