Solution sets of linear system and 2x2 determinant

The previous lecture covered the basics of 2x2 linear systems.

It is possible for a linear system to have no solutions at all or to have infinitely many solutions. In this lecture we will study these slightly more complicated cases and introduce the concept of determinant.

Slightly more complicated cases

Find the solution set to \[ \left\{ \begin{aligned} x + 2y &= 4 \\ 2x + 4y &= 3 . \end{aligned} \right. \]

It is easy to see that the two equations in this system contradict with one another, and therefore this system no solutions at all, i.e., the solution set is an empty set. Systems like this are said to be inconsistent.

Find the solution set to \[ \left\{ \begin{aligned} x + 2y &= 4 \\ 2x + 4y &= 8 . \end{aligned} \right. \]

We can see that if a choice of $x$ and $y$ satisfies the first equation, then the second equation is also satisfied. Therefore any $x$ and $y$ such that $x + 2y = 4$ would be a solution. There are infinitely many of them.

These examples showed three possibilities for the number of solutions.
  • A unique solution
  • Infinitely many solutions
  • No solutions at all

Determinant (2x2 case)

It is easy to distinguish the case of having unique solution and the two other cases (infinitely many solutions or no solutions at all).

One thing that can help us to determine if a linear system has a unique solution is "determinant".

For the linear system \[ \left\{ \begin{aligned} a x + b y &= f_1 \\ c x + d y &= f_2 \end{aligned} \right. \] in the unknowns $x$ and $y$, its determinant is the value \[ ad-bc \]
  • If the determinant is nonzero then the system has a unique solution.
  • If the determinant is zero then the system either has infinitely many solutions or no solutions at all (empty solution set).

It is important to note that the value of the determinant does not directly distinguish between the cases of infinite and empty solution sets.

Examples

  • Non-zero determinant: the system has a unique solution.
  • Zero determinant: the system either has infinitely many or no solutions.
Determine if the linear system \[ \left\{ \begin{aligned} 1x + 2y &= 5 \\ 3x + 4y &= 6 . \end{aligned} \right. \] has a unique solution.

The determinant of this system is \[ 1 \cdot 4 - 2 \cdot 3 = 4 - 6 = -2. \]

We can therefore conclude that this system indeed has a unique solution, i.e., there is exactly one solution.

Determine if the linear system \[ \left\{ \begin{aligned} 1x + 2y &= 4 \\ 2x + 4y &= 3 . \end{aligned} \right. \] has a unique solution.

The determinant of this system is \[ 1 \cdot 4 - 2 \cdot 2 = 4 - 4 = 0. \]

We can conclude that this system does not have a unique solution, i.e., it either has no solution at all or it has infinitely many solution.

Sidenote: the notation

Determinant is an important tool in mathematics. We will use it in several areas in linear algebra.

For the linear system \begin{align*} a x + b y &= f_1 \\ c x + d y &= f_2 \end{align*} in the unknowns $x$ and $y$, we may use the notation \[ \left| \begin{smallmatrix} a & b \\ c & d \end{smallmatrix} \right| \; = \; ad - bc \] for the determinant.

Even though this notation looks like the absolute value notation, we are not implying it is nonnegative. It is certainly possible for a determinant to be negative.

We also use the notation \[ \det \begin{bmatrix} a & b \\ c & d \end{bmatrix} \;=\; ad - bc \] for the determinant. This notation will make more sense once we study this in the context of matrices.

What does it mean?

It is easy to see the linear system \begin{align*} a x + b y &= f_1 \\ \color{red}{0} x + \color{red}{0} y &= f_2 \end{align*} either has no solution at all or has infinitely many solution.

Of course, the same applies if the linear system is of the form \begin{align*} \color{red}{0} x + \color{red}{0} y &= f_1 \\ c x + d y &= f_2 . \end{align*}

Or a system of the form \begin{align*} \color{red}{a} x + \color{blue}{b} y &= f_1 \\ \color{red}{a} x + \color{blue}{b} y &= f_2 . \end{align*}

We can see the determinant is designed to detect these situations. \[ \left| \begin{smallmatrix} a & b \\ 0 & 0 \end{smallmatrix} \right| \; = \; a0 - b0 \; = \; 0. \] The 0 determinant signals the system will not have a unique solution.

The same applies to the second case where the determinant is \[ \left| \begin{smallmatrix} 0 & 0 \\ c & d \end{smallmatrix} \right| \; = \; 0d - 0c \; = \; 0, \]

Similarly, for the third case, \[ \left| \begin{smallmatrix} a & b \\ a & b \end{smallmatrix} \right| \; = \; ab - ba \; = \; 0. \]
